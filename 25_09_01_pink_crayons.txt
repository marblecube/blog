                              PINK CRAYONS
                           by Vidal Palacios

INT. DOLLAR TREE – DAY

Fluorescent lights hum softly. The aisles are half-stocked, vaguely sticky. A
slow-motion pan catches VIDAL entering, trenchcoat flaring behind him like a
prophet at a discount apocalypse.

                              VIDAL (V.O.) 
                There’s no such thing as pink. Only light red.
                Pink is a social construct,invented to enforce
                gender norms, peddle toys and clothes, and 
                weaponize flowers in service of patriarchal
                authority.

He passes seasonal displays of Valentine's Day decorations. He sneers at them.

ANGLE ON: CRAYON SECTION.  Stacks of generic off-brand crayons. Pink
ones, vibrant and smug, peek through the cellophane.

                                 VIDAL
                                (softly)
                              Rot in hell.

He begins extracting each pink crayon one by one, methodically, like a surgeon
removing malignancies.

SNAP...SNAP...SNAP.

He looks up at the security camera and laughs as he shoves the broken sticks of
wax into his pockets and walks to the front of the store.

ANGLE ON: TRASH CAN NEAR EXIT.

He's holding a grip of bulging plastic bags in one hand and with his free one
he tosses the broken pink shards in with the gravity of someone discarding a
sacred lie.

He inhales deeply. Too deeply.  He stares into the middle distance, a single
nod betraying a profound sense of accomplishment.

                                                              FLASH FORWARD TO:

INT. MODEST LIVING ROOM – EVENING

A LITTLE GIRL sits at the coffee table. She colors carefully.

We see her drawing: CINDERELLA.

Most of it’s finished. The hair is yellow, the shoes silver, the sky in perfect
blue scribbles.

But her dress…her cheeks…blank.

She rifles through her crayon box, digging faster, growing panicked.

                                                                SMASH CUT TO:

VIDAL IN HIS PRIUS – EVENING

Parked in the dark, staring ahead, engine off.

                                                                    BACK TO: 

INT. MODEST LIVING ROOM – EVENING (CONTINUOUS)

Her eyes dart, a small crease forming between her brows. Her breath catches.

She looks up. Her face—pure, puzzled. The kind of heartbreak that doesn’t cry…
just lingers.

She bites her lip. A tear wells.  The magic is broken.

                                 VIDAL
                              (soft V.O.)
                    You’ll thank me in thirty years.

                                                                    BACK TO:

DOLLAR TREE PARKING LOT - EVENING (CONTINUOUS)

Vidal's car pulls out of the lot and merges into the flow of traffic,
disappearing into the quiet sprawl.

INT. SHARED APARTMENT – EVENING

A Valentine's Day commercial plays on the television

VIDAL is pacing the living room, his ROOMMATE sits at a table with his laptop
eating a bowl of milk and cornflakes. He's used to this now.

                                 VIDAL
            	  ...and that's why pink isn’t real. It’s just
                  light red. It was invented to push gender
                  roles. I mean, think about it; every toy 
                  aisle, every stupid Valentine’s Day display--

                                ROOMMATE 
                      (not looking up from laptop)
                 Yeah, but you know pink used to be a boys’
                 color, right?

                                 VIDAL 
                                (blinks)
                                 What?

                                ROOMMATE
                Like, historically. Early 20th century. Blue 
                was for girls,pink was seen as bold, active.
                There’s a whole Smithsonian article on it.
                You want me to pull it up?

                                 VIDAL
                                 Wait.

                                ROOMMATE
                            (already typing)
                Too late. (reading) 'In the early 1900s,pink 
                was considered more suitable for boys due to 
                its association with red, which was seen as 
                a strong, masculine color.’

                                 VIDAL
                           …That’s not real.

                                ROOMMATE
                          It’s from a museum.

VIDAL quietly unraveling as he slowly lowers himself into the couch.

EXT. MOUNTAIN CAMPSITE – NIGHT

A Prius pulls into a turnout. The headlights sweep across trees and disappear
as the engine cuts. The city's lights sparkle in the distance.

Vidal steps out. He walks around to the back and opens the hatch.

He begins unloading heart-shaped Valentine’s candy boxes. Dozens.  He sets them
down in a pile.

He adjusts the arrangement. He drenches them in starter fluid, and lights an
entire Dollar Tree matchbook before tossing it in.

The pile catches.

He unfolds a lawn chair and sits.

From a tote bag, he takes out a wire coat hanger and straightens it.

He pulls out a pack of hot dogs. Removes one. Skewers it.

He leans forward and holds it over the fire.

A candy heart pops in the flames.  He doesn’t move.

The fire burns.  The city glows faintly in the distance.
